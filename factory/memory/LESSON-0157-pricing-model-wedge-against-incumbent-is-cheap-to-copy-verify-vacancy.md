**Situation:** a discovery deep-dive on a "one-time-payment resume builder, anti-Zety-subscription"
idea assumed the incumbent's subscription trap (Zety can't cannibalize its own recurring revenue by
offering a one-time price) made the wedge safe. Research found the honest one-time-price angle was
ALREADY sold by at least three competitors (Rezi $149, JobSprout $99, Careerkit $129), plus SEO content
sites already ranking for the exact "no subscription resume builder" keyword.

**Lesson:** "the incumbent can't cannibalize their own pricing model" is true but doesn't make a
price/model wedge a moat — it only explains why the INCUMBENT won't compete on it, not why a fast new
entrant hasn't already. A pricing-model differentiator is cheap to copy and cheap to enter (no product
innovation required, just a different price sheet), so it attracts exactly the kind of small/indie
competitor who WILL take it, and often already has.

**Apply next time:** before treating a pricing/model wedge as a moat, explicitly name who ALREADY
occupies that exact wedge (search the literal claim — "no subscription `<category>`", "one-time payment
`<category>`" — plus the SEO/content layer, not just direct competitors) and verify it is genuinely
vacant, not merely un-targeted by the biggest incumbent.
