**Situation:** two corrections on the same case-study copy pass, from the same owner: (1) a draft leaned
on clever paradoxes and jargon left unexplained — rejected in favor of explaining each technical term
inside the sentence that uses it and preferring concrete numbers ("the screen said 300, the database said
250") over an aphorism; (2) a draft confessed "I'd do it differently" after almost every decision (three
in a row) — rejected as reading like pessimism rather than honesty; the correct cadence is exactly ONE
honest confession per case study, with the other decisions led by the win.

**Lesson:** both corrections are the same underlying taste applied to two different copy mechanics —
favor CONCRETE, LEGIBLE, CONFIDENT prose over cleverness-as-proof-of-skill. Jargon and paradox read as
showing off rather than explaining; over-hedging (a confession per decision) reads as insecurity rather
than honesty. A case study earns credibility by being clear and mostly showing wins, with rare, well-placed
honesty about a real misstep — not by demonstrating cleverness or by hedging every claim.

**Apply next time:** when drafting first-person case-study/portfolio copy: explain every technical term
inline in the sentence that uses it (never assume the reader knows the term), prefer a concrete
before/after number over a rhetorical flourish, and budget exactly ONE "I'd do it differently" confession
per case study — lead every other decision with the win, not a hedge.
